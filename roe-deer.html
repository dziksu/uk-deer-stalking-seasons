Quick Description

Roe Deer are small, elegant native deer found across woodland edges and farmland throughout Britain. Unlike every other UK deer species, they rut in high summer, then delay implantation of the embryo until winter so fawns are still born in the following spring. Solitary and territorial for most of the year, they're the species most UK stalkers cut their teeth on.

More About Roe Deer

Appearance

Roe Deer have a reddish-brown summer coat that turns greyish in winter, a black "moustache" stripe above a white chin patch, and a pale rump patch instead of a visible tail. Bucks carry small, upright antlers with a distinctively rough, pearled base and typically three points, cast in autumn and regrown through winter.

Behaviour & Social Structure

Roe are largely solitary or found in small family groups, and bucks hold and defend individual territories rather than gathering harems. Uniquely among British deer, their rut takes place in July and August; the fertilised egg then pauses its development through a process called delayed implantation, only implanting in the womb around January so that fawns are still born the following May or June.

Distribution & History

Roe Deer are one of Britain's oldest native species, though they died out across much of England by the 18th century before reintroductions and natural recolonisation restored them to most of the country. They're now common across England and Scotland but have no natural presence in Northern Ireland or Ireland.
