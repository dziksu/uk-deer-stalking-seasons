Quick Description

Fallow Deer are the UK's only species with broad, flattened "palmate" antlers, resembling an open hand. Introduced by the Normans nearly a thousand years ago for park hunting, they're now widespread and highly gregarious, often seen in large mixed herds. Their coats vary dramatically — from classic spotted chestnut to solid black or near-white — making individual recognition part of the fun of watching them.

Good to Know for Stalking

🌳
Woodland & parkland habitsFallow are most often stalked in woodland and old deer parks, so shots tend to be shorter range than open-hill Red Deer stalking, but cover makes spotting harder.
🎤
Groaning bucks hold "stands"During the October rut, bucks defend traditional rutting stands and make a deep groaning/belching call — a reliable way to locate mature males without a long approach.
👀
Confirm your target within the herdFallow live in large mixed-sex herds for much of the year, so take time to positively identify sex and maturity before taking a shot when several animals are grouped together.
🎨
Coat colour isn't a reliable age guideMenil, black (melanistic) and white colour morphs occur naturally alongside the common spotted coat — don't use coat colour alone to judge age or cull priority.

More About Fallow Deer

Appearance

The most common Fallow coat is chestnut with white spots and a black-bordered white rump patch shaped like an inverted horseshoe, but menil (paler, spotted year-round), melanistic (near-black) and leucistic (near-white) forms are all frequently seen in the same herd. Bucks grow their distinctive palmate antlers from their second year, with the palm broadening each year until maturity.

Behaviour & Social Structure

Fallow Deer are the most gregarious UK deer, typically living in single-sex herds that can number into the hundreds in large parks. During the rut, bucks either defend a traditional stand and call to attract does, or gather in a "lek" of closely spaced stands where does can compare several males at once.

Distribution & History

Fallow Deer were introduced to Britain by the Normans in the 11th century specifically to stock hunting parks, though there's evidence of earlier Roman introductions that didn't persist. Escapees and deliberate releases from parkland estates over the centuries have established free-living populations across most of England, with smaller pockets in Wales and Scotland.
