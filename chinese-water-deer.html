Quick Description

Chinese Water Deer are the UK's smallest deer alongside Muntjac, and the only species where neither sex grows antlers — instead, males have visible curved tusks earning them the nickname "vampire deer." Introduced from China in the early 20th century, the UK now holds a globally important share of the world population, since the species is threatened in its native range.

More About Chinese Water Deer

Appearance

Chinese Water Deer have a rounded, "teddy bear" face with large dark eyes and prominent rounded ears, and a plain sandy-brown coat with no spots or rump patch. Males lack antlers entirely but grow noticeably long canine tusks that protrude below the upper lip, used in fights over territory rather than for feeding.

Behaviour & Social Structure

Unlike most UK deer, Chinese Water Deer are solitary and strongly territorial year-round, with males using their tusks in disputes rather than any antler-based sparring. The rut takes place in November and December, and does typically give birth to unusually large litters for a deer — commonly two or three fawns, occasionally more.

Distribution & History

Introduced to Woburn and Whipsnade in the early 20th century, escapees established a free-living population concentrated in the wetlands and farmland of East Anglia and Bedfordshire. The species is not present in Northern Ireland, Wales, or Scotland in any meaningful numbers, and its UK open season dates apply to England and Wales only. Because Chinese Water Deer are considered vulnerable in their native range, the UK's population is of real international conservation significance.
