Quick Description

Muntjac are the UK's smallest deer, introduced from China in the early 1900s and now rapidly expanding across England and into Wales. Uniquely among British deer, they breed continuously year-round rather than following a fixed autumn rut, which is why the law gives them no close season anywhere in the UK — though ethical culling still avoids does with dependent young.

Good to Know for Stalking

📅
Year-round management is normalWith no close season, Muntjac control happens throughout the year — but BASC and the British Deer Society still advise only culling immature animals, bucks, or heavily pregnant does.
🚫👶
Avoid does with dependent youngBecause muntjac breed continuously, a doe can be nursing a fawn at almost any time of year — take extra care not to orphan a dependent fawn when selecting a target.
🐕
The bark is a giveawayTerritorial muntjac give a loud, repeated bark that carries through woodland — useful for locating an animal, but stay patient since they're small and easily hidden in cover.
🏡
Increasingly found near gardensTheir rapid range expansion means muntjac now turn up in gardens and urban fringe woodland — always be certain of your backstop given the more built-up settings they're found in.

More About Muntjac Deer

Appearance

Muntjac have a hunched stance with a raised rump higher than the shoulders, and a rich chestnut coat. Bucks grow short, simple single-point antlers on long pedicles and visible tusk-like canines, while does have small bony knobs on the head instead of antlers along with their own smaller tusks.

Behaviour & Social Structure

Muntjac are solitary and strongly territorial, marking territory with scent glands and defending it with a loud, repeated bark — earning them the nickname "barking deer." Unlike every other UK deer species, they have no fixed breeding season: does can conceive again within days of giving birth, so fawns are born throughout the year rather than in a single spring flush.

Distribution & History

Reeves's Muntjac were introduced to Woburn Abbey from China in the early 1900s, and escapes and deliberate releases over the following decades fuelled a rapid spread across England. They continue to expand their range into Wales and further north, and are widely regarded as an invasive species due to the browsing damage they cause to woodland understorey and ground flora such as bluebells.
